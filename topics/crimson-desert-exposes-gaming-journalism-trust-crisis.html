Are Game Reviews Broken? How Crimson Desert Exposed a Trust Crisis in Gaming Journalism

By Nwezeaku Clinton | Updated April 16, 2026

Table of Contents

Wait… Are Reviews Actually Broken? 😏

Let’s start with a simple question. If a game is selling millions, topping charts, and players are glued to their screens… but critics say it’s “meh”— Who’s wrong? Because right now, thanks to Crimson Desert, that exact situation is playing out—and it’s messy.

Like “group chat argument at 2AM” messy.

And suddenly, everyone is asking the same thing:

“Can we even trust game reviews anymore?”

The Moment Everything Got Awkward

So here’s what happened. A big game drops. Critics give it a lukewarm score. Cool, nothing new. Then… players show up. In huge numbers. Like, “servers sweating” numbers 🔥

Now the internet is confused. Because usually, critics and players don’t agree perfectly—but they don’t clash like this either.

This felt different. This felt like:

  • Critics: “It’s not that good.”
  • Players: “I literally can’t stop playing.”

That’s not just disagreement, that’s two completely different realities.

The whole "Critic" Error, caused a huge controversy when crimson desert announced it's sales success.

Why People Are Side-Eyeing Reviews Now

Let’s break down why trust is… let’s say “under maintenance.”

  • Timing pressure — Review needs to drop fast. No time to fully explore. Speed > depth.
  • Different playstyles — Critics might rush content. Players take their time and actually enjoy it.
  • Expectation bias — If a game doesn’t match the “vision,” it gets punished.
  • Score inflation chaos — One game gets a 9, another gets a 6, and you’re sitting there like… how??

Meanwhile in real life: it’s like someone reviewing a buffet after eating only the salad.

Technically valid… but also deeply suspicious.

The “Expert vs Player” Problem

Here’s where things get interesting.

Critics are supposed to be experts. But players are the ones actually living in the game world for hours.

So when opinions clash, people naturally ask:

“Who’s more qualified here?”

And lately, the answer has been shifting toward:

“…the people actually playing the game.”

Wild, right?

It’s like a movie critic saying a film is boring while the audience is giving it a standing ovation.

At some point, you stop listening to the critic and start trusting the crowd.

Numbers don't lie, so this begs the question, Steam Charts vs Critics: Who Really Decides a Game’s Success in 2026?. This starts as a debate, we all have different tastes anyways. but then... numbers don't lie.

Let’s Talk About That “Re-Review” Energy

Now here’s the part that really raised eyebrows.

After backlash, some critics hinted at wanting to revisit their scores.

And look—updating opinions isn’t bad. We all change our minds. But doing it after the internet drags you?

That’s where things start looking… convenient.

It’s like failing a test, seeing everyone else pass, then asking:

“Can I retake it? I think I misunderstood the questions.”

😅

So… What Are Reviews Actually Good For?

Before we cancel reviews completely (relax, we’re not doing that), let’s be fair.

They still have value.

  • Early warning system — “Hey, this game is broken.” Useful.
  • Technical insight — Performance, bugs, weird issues.
  • Different perspective — Sometimes they notice things you don’t.

But here’s the key difference now:

They’re no longer the final word.

They’re just one voice in a very loud conversation.

What Gamers Are Doing Instead

Players have basically built their own review system.

  • Watch gameplay clips
  • Check player counts
  • Read user reviews
  • Ask friends

It’s like assembling your own Avengers team of opinions.

Meanwhile critics are like:

“Guys… remember us?”

😏

The Real Issue: Trust, Not Scores

Here’s the thing nobody wants to admit.

This isn’t really about numbers. Not the 6/10. Not the 9/10. It’s about trust.

When players feel like reviews don’t reflect reality, they stop listening. Simple as that.

And once trust is gone? Good luck getting it back.

That’s not a bug. That’s a full system crash.

Final Thoughts (And a Tiny Reality Check)

So… are game reviews broken?

Not completely. But they’re definitely not what they used to be.

Crimson Desert didn’t destroy reviews—it exposed the cracks that were already there.

And now everyone sees them.

⚠️ Quick warning: don’t swing too far the other way. Just because critics can be wrong doesn’t mean the crowd is always right. The internet has terrible taste sometimes. (Yes, I said it.)

Balance is still your best friend.

Now I’ve got to ask—

Do you still trust game reviews, or are you fully relying on gameplay and player opinions now?

Let’s talk.
